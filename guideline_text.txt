2.0 Introduction 
In computer science and computer engineering research, a literature review is  a structured 
analysis of previously published research related to a specific technical problem. Rather than 
simply summarizing earlier studies, the literature review critically evaluates existing 
algorithms, technologies, system architectures, and methodologies  to understand how 
researchers have attempted to solve the problem. 
The main purpose of this chapter is to: 
• demonstrate understanding of existing technologies and research 
• analyse previous solutions and their limitations 
• identify gaps in current knowledge 
• justify the need for the proposed system or solution 
In computing disciplines, the literature review must focus on technical analysis . Students 
should examine how different systems work, compare their performance, and evaluate their 
strengths and weaknesses. 
This chapter , therefore, reviews existing research related to the specific project topic , 
examining both traditional and modern approaches used to solve the identified problem. The 
chapter concludes by identifying a research gap and introducing the conceptual direction of 
the proposed solution. 
Important guideline 
The introduction must immediately state the actual project topic . Avoid generic statements 
such as “this chapter reviews literature related to the topic.” 
Example: 
Instead of writing: 
This chapter reviews literature related to network security. 
Write: 
This chapter reviews existing intrusion detection systems designed for small -office 
networks and analyses their effectiveness in detecting cyber threats.

2.4 Research Gap 
After reviewing previous research, the next step is to identify what still remains unsolved. 
This is known as the research gap. 
Despite the significant progress in systems designed to address [problem], several limitations 
still exist. 
For example: 
• Many systems focus primarily on accuracy, ignoring real-time performance. 
• Some solutions require large datasets  that may not be available in practical 
environments. 
• Other systems rely on high-performance hardware, making them unsuitable for low-
cost deployment. 
Additionally, many existing solutions are designed for controlled experimental 
environments, making them difficult to deploy in real-world conditions. 
Therefore, there is a need for a system that: 
• improves system performance 
• reduces computational complexity 
• supports real-time processing 
• remains scalable and cost-effective 
This unresolved problem forms the foundation of the present research.

Research design refers to the overall strategy used to integrate the different components of 
the study coherently and logically . It ensures that the research problem is addressed 
effectively. 
In computing research, the research design often combines several approaches, including: 
• Experimental research 
• Design science research 
• Simulation-based research 
• Case study analysis 
• Data-driven research 
The choice of research design depends on the nature of the research problem, the type of system 
being developed, and the objectives of the study.

3.2 Research Methods 
This section explains the research methods used in the study. 
3.2.1 Empirical / Experimental Method 
The empirical method is a research approach that concludes observable and measurable 
evidence. In computing research, empirical methods are commonly used to evaluate systems, 
algorithms, and technologies through experimentation. 
Researchers conduct controlled experiments to test hypotheses and evaluate system 
performance. 
This method is commonly applied in areas such as: 
• Artificial neural networks (e.g., implementing new social networks using concepts from 
existing networks) 
• Automated theorem proving 
• Natural language processing 
• Performance and behaviour analysis of systems 
• Human–Computer Interaction (HCI) 
Experimental evaluation is typically divided into two phases: 
1. Exploratory Phase 
During this phase, the researcher collects measurements and observations to identify important 
questions about the system under investigation. 
2. Evaluation Phase 
In this phase, experiments are conducted to answer the questions identified in the exploratory 
phase. 
4 
 
A well-designed experiment begins with a clearly defined list of research questions that the 
experiment aims to answer. 
Experiments may be conducted to study the behaviour of: 
• Natural systems (e.g., physics-based simulations) 
• Humans and organizations (e.g., experimental psychology) 
• Artificial systems (e.g., algorithm performance evaluation) 
Experiments may involve: 
• Performance benchmarking 
• Usability testing 
• System comparison 
3.2.2 Computer Simulation Method 
The computer simulation method is used to study complex phenomena that may be difficult, 
impractical, or too expensive to investigate through real-world experimentation. 
Computer simulations allow researchers to model systems and evaluate their behaviour 
under different conditions. 
This method is particularly useful for investigating systems that are outside the experimental 
domain. 
Common application areas include: 
• Computer networks (e.g., developing and testing new communication protocols) 
• Computer hardware architectures 
• Traffic flow simulation 
• Distributed systems modelling 
• Virtual reality simulations 
Through simulation models, researchers can evaluate system performance, predict behaviour, 
and analyse outcomes without implementing the system in a real-world environment. 
Simulation tools such as NS-3, OMNeT++, MATLAB, or Python simulation frameworks  
are often used. 
 
 
 
 
5 
 
3.2.3 Theoretical Method / Formal Methods and Mathematical Proof 
The theoretical research method is based on logic, mathematics, and formal reasoning. It is 
used to develop algorithms, computational models, and formal proofs. 
In this approach, researchers use mathematical techniques to demonstrate that a system 
possesses certain desired properties. 
Typical outputs of theoretical research include: 
• Conceptual models 
• Mathematical models 
• Algorithms 
• Formal proofs 
Common techniques used include: 
• Iteration 
• Recursion 
• Complexity analysis 
• Mathematical induction 
For example, when developing a new machine learning classifier , researchers may use 
mathematical modelling to prove the efficiency and correctness of the model. 
Theoretical methods are widely used in areas such as: 
• Computational complexity theory 
• Algorithm design 
• Formal logic systems 
• Artificial intelligence theory 
 
3.2.4 Design Science 
Design science is a research methodology commonly used in information systems and 
software engineering. The primary objective is to create innovative artefacts that solve 
practical problems. 
Artefacts produced in design science research may include: 
• Software systems 
• Algorithms 
• Data models 
6 
 
• Hardware models 
• Databases 
• Frameworks 
• Methodologies 
Design science research is often referred to as constructive research because it involves 
building and evaluating new solutions based on theoretical principles. 
A build methodology  involves constructing an artefact to demonstrate that a solution is 
feasible. 
For the research to qualify as a contribution, the artefact must: 
• Introduce an innovation, or 
• Contain new features not previously demonstrated 
Design science emphasises systematic evaluation and iterative improvement of artefacts to 
ensure they meet intended objectives. 
The design science process usually involves: 
1. Problem identification 
2. Artefact design 
3. Artefact development 
4. Artefact evaluation 
5. Iterative improvement 
This methodology has become increasingly important as organisations seek improved 
information systems design to avoid costly IT failures and poor data management.

Common AI techniques include:

3.2.6 Data Mining 
Data mining is the process of extracting hidden, previously unknown, and potentially 
useful information from large datasets. 
It integrates methods from: 
• Database systems 
• Machine learning 
• Artificial intelligence 
• Statistics 
Typical data mining tasks include: 
• Classification 
• Clustering 
• Association rule mining 
• Anomaly detection 
Data mining is widely applied in: 
• Business analytics 
• Fraud detection 
• Customer behaviour analysis 
8 
 
• Cybersecurity 
Data mining techniques can also be applied to address various data quality issues. 
For example: 
• Outlier detection algorithms can identify anomalies in datasets. 
• Data cleansing techniques can improve data quality. 
• Intrusion detection systems can identify suspicious activities. 
Data mining is also widely used in schema matching, where algorithms identify relationships 
between different database schemas. 
Although many data mining algorithms are robust, special handling may be required when 
working with datasets containing data quality issues.

4.7 General Comments  
4.7.1 Consistency is Everything 
Requirements → Design → Implementation must align perfectly.

4.7.2 Avoid Over-Engineering

4.7.3 Justify Every Decision 
Every choice should answer: 
• Why this approach? 
• Why not alternatives? 
• Why this algorithm? 
• Why this architecture? 
• Why this tool? 
4.7.4 Think Like an Engineer, Not a Narrator 
A narrator describes what happened.

• Why it works 
• Under what conditions it fails 
• How it can scale 
4.7.5 Overpromising vs Implementation Reality 
Students often describe: 
“AI-powered intelligent system”

A basic if-else rule system
