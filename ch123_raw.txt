ABSTRACT
Standard residential Distribution Boards in Zimbabwe are passive devices. They trip on dangerous faults. They do nothing else. When a prepaid token balance reaches zero ,all power cuts instantly.  No load is prioritised and no appliance is protected. The refrigerator loses power at the exact same moment as the electric iron.
This research designs, implement  and evaluates an automated energy triage system built on an ESP32 microcontroller. The system uses SCT-013 non invasive current transformer sensors to monitor residential electrical loads in real time. It classifies each load into Tier 1 (Essential), Tier 2 (Comfort) or Tier 3 (Heavy Drain) based on measured power consumption.
When the energy budget falls below a configurable critical threshold, the system autonomously dispatches low voltage commands to an isolated external relay module to shed Tier 3 loads first, then Tier 2 loads if required, all while preserving continuous power to all Tier 1 appliances. This physical separation ensures that high voltage switching transients do not interfere with the microcontroller's operation.
The system is evaluated using controlled load scenarios on a prototype circuit ,measuring classification accuracy, relay response latency, budget tracking error and graceful degradation behaviour. A local Wi-Fi dashboard provides real time monitoring without requiring a mobile app. This study contributes the first validated ,open residential energy monitoring design specifically targeting the Zimbabwean market.

CHAPTER 1: INTRODUCTION
1.1 Background of the Study
ZESA Holdings and its operational subsidiary , the Zimbabwe Electricity Transmission and Distribution Company (ZETDC) , have implemented load shedding schedules of between 10 hours and 19 hours daily in many residential areas since 2019. Kariba Dam undercapacity and an ageing national grid drive this shortage.
ZESA prepaid token metering has become the dominant billing mechanism for grid-connected homes in Zimbabwe. This introduces a critical characteristic to residential energy management: a strict, finite energy budget. A token purchase provides a fixed quantity of kilowatt hours that continuously depletes with use. The household is forced to manage a constantly diminishing resource, yet there is currently no device in the standard electrical distribution board capable of managing this budget intelligently.
The standard residential Distribution Board (DB) was designed for one purpose: to trip when a dangerous overcurrent fault occurs. It does nothing else. It has no awareness of token balances , battery states or load priorities. It sits passive and unbothered until everything goes dark at once.
This gap between the finite nature of residential energy budgets and the passivity of the electrical infrastructure is the engineering problem that this research addresses.Smart home energy management systems exist in Europe and North America. They require reliable internet ,compatible smart meters and budgets that exclude most Zimbabwean homes. They are also cloud dependent which means they fail precisely when the power goes out and the router goes offline.
This research proposes a different approach. Using an ESP32 microcontroller ,SCT-013 current transformer sensors and relay modules , the system monitors household loads in real time , classifies them by priority and autonomously disconnects appliances  according to configured priority ,  before the budget reaches zero. Everything runs on the ESP32. No cloud, no subscription, no internet required.


1.2 Problem Statement
The core problem is that , the residential electrical infrastructure in Zimbabwe is passive. The Distribution Board responds only to overcurrent faults. It has no intelligence, no memory and it has no budget awareness.
When a ZESA prepaid balance reaches zero, the meter cuts all power simultaneously. The refrigerator loses power at the same moment as the electric iron. A household that could have preserved essential power for two more hours by shedding non-essential loads instead, it experiences a complete blackout. This is a failure of intelligence not technology.
Load awareness is completely absent from standard residential systems. A clamp meter tells you how many amps flow through a wire. It cannot tell you whether those amps come from a geyser, a kettle or a combination of smaller loads. Identifying appliances from their power signatures ,a field called Non-Intrusive Load Monitoring (NILM) , has been studied extensively but always on cloud-based hardware far more powerful than a microcontroller.
No affordable autonomous triage device exists in the Zimbabwean market. Smart plugs cost 15 to 40 USD per outlet , require Wi-Fi and offer no autonomous decision making. Programmable timers use fixed schedules .Solar charge controllers cut everything at a fixed threshold . None can selectively shed a geyser while keeping a refrigerator powered.
There is a clear need for a system that monitors loads in real time using affordable non-invasive sensors, classifies them into priority tiers, tracks the energy budget continuously and executes graduated triage shedding before the budget reaches zero. It must work offline, fit within microcontroller constraints and cost less than 50 USD. No existing product in Zimbabwe satisfies all of these requirements.
1.3 Research Aim and Objectives
1.3.1 Research Aim
To design an automated energy system that monitors residential electrical loads in real time before the energy budget reaches a critical level without any dependency on cloud infrastructure or internet connectivity.
1.3.2 Research Objectives
To develop a real time, tiered load classification model for residential electrical appliances. 
To implement an autonomous energy budget tracking module.
To design a priority-based triage decision engine for automated load shedding.
To develop a localized, offline monitoring dashboard for real-time system visibility.

1.4 Significance of the Study
This research matters most to the household that woke up this morning to find the refrigerator warm and the battery bank dead. That happens daily , in tens of thousands of Zimbabwean homes and it is almost entirely preventable with the right intelligence in the electrical system.
This study contributes a validated implementation of real time load classification and autonomous triage running entirely on an ESP32 microcontroller. The existing literature on residential energy management assumes cloud connectivity and hardware far more powerful than a microcontroller. This research proves that meaningful load-aware energy management is achievable at the edge.
This research produces a hardware design ,firmware architecture and validated performance baseline that a Zimbabwean manufacturer or solar energy company could use as the foundation of a commercial product.
Every household using ZESA prepaid tokens is a potential customer. At the social level, this research directly addresses budget preservation and food security. A household that exhausts its ZESA token prematurely due to an active geyser loses refrigeration, risking food spoilage. By autonomously shedding heavy loads, this system stretches the finite token budget, preserving essential power for critical needs.
This research also contributes to Chinhoyi University of Technology's mandate to produce engineering graduates whose research addresses applied problems of national relevance. This dissertation provides a reproducible and evaluable technical artefact in that tradition.
1.5 Limitations and Delimitations
1.5.1 Limitations
Single-Phase Measurement Only. The prototype measures a single phase AC circuit. Three phase residential configurations ,present in some larger Zimbabwean properties  are outside the current scope. The architecture supports multi-channel expansion through hardware additions.
Tier Classification versus Appliance Identification. The system classifies loads by power threshold , not individual appliance identity. A 2,800-watt load is Tier 3 regardless of whether it is a geyser or a workshop tool. Appliance specific rules require a more complex classification approach beyond this dissertation's scope.
Simulated Budget Scenarios. Energy budget tracking is validated against controlled simulation rather than a live ZESA prepaid meter or commercial battery management system. Real world depletion dynamics may introduce additional variables not present in the test environment.
Prototype Scale. The prototype is assembled on a circuit board with controlled test loads. Real residential wiring introduces electromagnetic interference and harmonic distortions that may affect CT sensor accuracy beyond controlled test levels.
1.5.2 Delimitations
This study focuses exclusively on residential electrical load management. Commercial and industrial electrical systems are outside scope. The triage algorithm uses three defined tiers. Full NILM is explicitly outside scope.
The prototype monitors up to four independent circuits. The system is designed and evaluated for Android accessible browser dashboards only. iOS specific app development is outside scope.
The research evaluates a defined set of test appliances. Generalisation beyond the tested scenarios should be validated in future deployment studies.
1.5.3 Mitigation Plans
To address prototype scale limitations , the evaluation uses resistive and inductive test loads that reproduce real appliance current waveform characteristics. This provides controlled but realistic signal conditions for calibration.
To address the simulated budget limitation, a simulated linear token depletion model is implemented and validated against calculated ground  values derived from measured current readings.
To address the single phase limitation, the firmware architecture natively supports multi-channel ADC reading. Extension to three-phase systems requires only hardware additions and minor firmware configuration.
Furthermore, to mitigate high voltage electromagnetic interference (EMI) disrupting the low voltage sensor readings, the system employed a physically isolated modular architecture. Rather than placing high current 220V relays on the primary processing board , the ESP32 brain and the CT sensor circuits are isolated. Relay switching commands are dispatched via a low voltage ribbon cable to a completely separate, external relay module. This physical separation prevents relay coil transients from corrupting the continuous RMS current calculations
1.6 Research Questions
This study is directed by the following research questions, which examine both technical performance and practical feasibility in the Zimbabwean residential electrical context.
Can an ESP32 with an SCT-013 CT sensor accurately measure RMS current to within 5%  of a reference clamp meter reading across the operating range of Zimbabwean residential circuits?
Can a real -time load classification algorithm running on the ESP32 correctly assign detected loads to Tier 1, Tier 2 or Tier 3 with 85 % or higher accuracy across a defined set of common household appliances?
Does the on device energy budget tracking module maintain cumulative tracking error below 5% over a 4 hour test window when calibrated against known reference loads?
Does the triage decision engine execute a relay shed command within 500 milliseconds of detecting a budget threshold breach while maintaining continuous power to all Tier 1 loads?
Does the local Wi-Fi dashboard provide sufficiently accurate and timely information for a household user to monitor load status and energy budget without technical expertise in electrical engineering?
1.7 Research Hypotheses
Based on the problem statement and theoretical framework established in Chapter 2 , the following hypotheses guide implementation and evaluation.
The ESP32 with SCT-013 CT sensor will measure RMS current to within 5% of a reference clamp meter for loads between 0.5Amps  and 30Amps , representing the full operating range of a standard Zimbabwean residential circuit.
A threshold based load classification algorithm using real time RMS power as the primary discriminating feature will correctly classify loads into the three defined tiers with accuracy exceeding 85% , provided distinct power ranges exist between tiers.
The on device budget tracking module will maintain cumulative error below 5% over a 4 hour test window, because the primary error source is CT sensor noise, which is bounded by the ESP32's internal 12-bit ADC resolution.
Relay response time from threshold detection to physical relay activation will be below 500 milliseconds , because the ESP32 runs an event driven firmware loop with no OS scheduling latency and relay switching time is approximately 10 milliseconds.
Graceful degradation, meaning Tier 3 shed before Tier 2 with Tier 1 preserved throughout , will be confirmed in 100% of simulated budget critical test scenarios, demonstrating deterministic triage logic execution.

These hypotheses provide clear , measurable and falsifiable targets for the evaluation chapter. A rejected hypothesis is itself a research contribution by identifying a specific performance gap in the proposed architecture.

2.0 Introduction
This chapter reviews existing research on residential load monitoring, energy budget management, microcontroller-based relay control and edge computing for automated triage in resource constrained electrical systems. The purpose is to examine previously proposed solutions , analyse their strengths and weaknesses, and identify gaps that justify the proposed system.
Recent advances in embedded computing and IoT sensing have enabled the development of systems capable of real time energy monitoring at the device level. Despite these advances, several critical challenges remain, particularly in real time load tier classification on constrained hardware, autonomous budget-aware shedding without cloud dependency and cost-effective deployment in sub-Saharan African residential contexts.
Researchers have proposed approaches including schedule-based relay controllers, cloud connected smart home monitors ,neural network NILM classifiers and voltage threshold battery management systems. While these approaches demonstrate value in specific contexts , they present limitations in performance ,cloud dependency, hardware cost or deployment complexity that make them unsuitable for the Zimbabwean residential market.
This chapter reviews and analyses these approaches to identify the research gap motivating the present study. The chapter is organized as follows: Section 2.1 presents the theoretical background. Section 2.2 reviews existing approaches across three subsections. Section 2.3 provides a comparative analysis. Section 2.4 identifies the research gap. 2.1 Theoretical Background
This section defines the key concepts and technologies necessary to understand the research problem and the proposed system.
2.1.1 Residential Electrical Load Management
Electrical load management refers to the process of monitoring , controlling and optimising the consumption of electrical energy within a defined supply system. In a residential context , load management aims to match energy consumption with available supply capacity or budget. It involves the identification of energy consuming devices and the application of rules or algorithms to control when and how those devices draw power (Wanjiru, Zhang and Xia, 2017).
Load management strategies are broadly classified into passive and active approaches. Passive strategies  such as standard MCB protection respond only to fault conditions and offer no intelligence. Active strategies apply programmable logic or algorithms to make real time decisions about which loads to shed  when to shed them and in what order. This research implements an active, tier based, autonomous load management strategy on an ESP32 microcontroller.
Load management is applied in domains including industrial demand response, grid scale frequency regulation and residential energy conservation. The key distinction of this research is the application of autonomous load management at the residential appliance level , using non-invasive sensing  without any external network infrastructure.
2.1.2 Non-Intrusive Load Monitoring (NILM)
Non-Intrusive Load Monitoring (NILM), also called energy disaggregation ,is the practice of inferring individual appliance power consumption from an aggregate household measurement without per appliance sensors (Hart, 1992). It uses electrical signature analysis to identify specific appliances based on their characteristic current waveforms, harmonic content or switching transients.
Modern NILM approaches use machine learning algorithms including Hidden Markov Models , Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks trained on labelled consumption datasets. Common benchmark datasets include REFIT and UK-DALE  which contain power consumption traces from UK households. These datasets do not reflect the appliance profiles of Zimbabwean households, where brands, wattage ratings and usage patterns differ significantly.
Full NILM requires substantial computational resources and appliance specific training data. This research adopts a simplified approach: tier based power threshold classification. Rather than identifying specific appliances, the system assigns loads to priority tiers based on the measured RMS power. This pragmatic simplification is tractable on an ESP32 and independent of appliance specific training data.
2.1.3 Current Transformer Sensing and RMS Measurement
A Current Transformer (CT) is a device that measures alternating current in a conductor through electromagnetic induction , without any galvanic connection to the measured circuit. The SCT-013 is a split-core CT sensor widely used in energy monitoring projects. It outputs a voltage signal proportional to the measured current through a built-in burden resistor (Sadiku and Alexander, 2021).
Root Mean Square (RMS) current is the effective value of an alternating current that produces the same power dissipation as an equivalent direct current. For a sinusoidal current, the RMS value equals the peak value divided by the square root of two. In practice ,RMS is calculated digitally by sampling the current waveform at sufficient frequency, squaring the samples, averaging and taking the square root. The ESP32's built in 12-bit ADC provides adequate resolution and high speed sampling  for RMS computation from the SCT-013 output when properly DC biased.
Real power in watts is derived from RMS current multiplied by supply voltage and power factor. For resistive loads such as geysers and kettles the power factor approaches unity, making the calculation straightforward. For inductive loads such as motors and compressors, the power factor must be estimated.. This research uses an estimated power factor of 0.95 for inductive loads as a practical approximation within the accuracy targets defined in Objective 1.
2.1.4 Edge Computing on Microcontrollers
Edge computing is a paradigm in which data processing occurs at or near the source of data generation, rather than in a centralised cloud server. On microcontrollers, edge computing enables real-time inference and control without network connectivity, with very low latency and at minimal power consumption (Banbury et al., 2021).
The ESP32 is a dual-core 32-bit microcontroller with built-in Wi-Fi and Bluetooth, operating at up to 240 MHz with 520 kilobytes of SRAM. It is the dominant platform for IoT edge applications in the Zimbabwean hobbyist and light industrial markets at approximately 5 USD retail. Its dual-core architecture allows one core to handle ADC sampling continuously while the other manages the Wi-Fi access point and dashboard server  ,preventing timing interference between these tasks.
FreeRTOS, the real-time operating system supported natively by the ESP32 Arduino framework, provides task scheduling ,priority assignment and inter-task communication primitives  .The triage decision engine runs as a high-priority FreeRTOS task that preempts the dashboard task when a budget threshold breach is detected, ensuring deterministic response times independent of dashboard HTTP request timing.
2.1.5 Relay-Based Autonomous Load Control
An electromechanical relay switches a high-voltage load circuit using a low-voltage electromagnetic coil. When the ESP32 drives a GPIO pin high, a transistor energises the relay coil, closing or opening the load contacts. The relay provides galvanic isolation between the 3.3-V control logic and the 230V AC load circuit, which is essential for electrical safety in residential installations.
Relay switching time , the duration from coil energisation to full contact closure, is typically 10 to 15 milliseconds for standard 5V relay modules. This switching time is negligible relative to the 500 millisecond end-to-end response target specified in Hypothesis 4. Contact ratings must match the load current: the 10Amp  rated relay modules used in this prototype are adequate for testing but would require 16Amp  or 20Amp rated alternatives for production geyser or washing machine circuits.
All relay wiring in a residential installation must comply with Zimbabwe Standards Board regulation ZW 02:2019. The ESP32 control circuit operates exclusively at 5 V DC and 3.3 V DC, providing full galvanic isolation from the mains supply when the relay module's optocoupler-based isolation is properly implemented.
2.2 Review of Existing Approaches
2.2.1 Traditional Approaches
Early residential energy management relied on passive protection devices and manual human intervention. Standard Distribution Boards equipped with Miniature Circuit Breakers (MCBs) are sized to protect wiring from dangerous overcurrent faults. Wanjiru, Zhang and Xia (2017) reviewed residential demand-side management strategies across Southern Africa and confirmed that passive MCB panels represent the universal starting point from which all intelligent energy management systems must improve. Their study found that no passive protection mechanism offers any form of budget awareness, load priority or graduated shedding capability.
Programmable timers represent the most common active intervention used by Zimbabwean households for geyser energy management. A timer activates the geyser during off-peak hours to reduce consumption. However, Chipfunde and Mutsvangwa (2023) conducted a survey of 120 Zimbabwean urban households and found that timer-based geyser management provided no protection against budget depletion during periods of higher-than-normal consumption. A timer set to activate from 5 AM to 7 AM will run regardless of whether the battery bank is at 90 % or 12 % state of charge. This inflexibility makes timers fundamentally unsuitable as a dynamic budget management tool.
ZESA prepaid token meters provide energy budget awareness in the sense that they display the remaining balance on a small LCD. However, Makonese et al. (2022) reviewed smart energy monitoring adoption across South African households and found that token meter displays require active human monitoring and provide no automated response capability. When the balance reaches zero, all circuits are cut simultaneously with no load prioritisation. This binary all-or-nothing behaviour is the specific failure mode this research targets.
These traditional approaches established the performance baseline for residential energy management in Zimbabwe but share a fundamental limitation: they offer no load aware, graduated ,autonomous shedding capability. Their limitations prompted researchers to explore intelligent and data driven approaches ,as reviewed in the following section.
2.2.2 Intelligent and Data-Driven Systems
Recent research has increasingly applied intelligent computing techniques to residential energy management. IoT connectivity, embedded machine learning and data driven decision engines have been explored to address the limitations of passive and schedule-based approaches.
Diarra et al. (2023) proposed a microcontroller based energy management system for off grid solar applications in West Africa. Their system used an Arduino Mega and a custom relay controller to implement fixed schedule load shedding triggered by battery voltage thresholds. A field trial across 45 households in Senegal and Mali achieved a 23% battery life extension compared to unmanaged operation. This result is significant and directly relevant to the Zimbabwean context. However, the system used battery voltage as a proxy for state of charge, introducing inaccuracy due to the non-linear voltage SoC relationship of lead acid batteries. Load control was schedule-based rather than responsive to real time power consumption, meaning the system could not react to unexpected high-drain load activation outside the scheduled periods.
Osei-Owusu et al. (2024) implemented a single relay load management system using an ESP8266 microcontroller for residential solar applications in Ghana. The system monitored total household current consumption and shed one relay controlled load when consumption exceeded a certain threshold. Relay response time averaged 380 milliseconds which established a relevant performance benchmark for this research. The limitation was that the system treated all the less essential loads identically and could not prioritise shedding based on load tier. A geyser and a mobile phone charger would both trigger the same relay response regardless of their relative contribution to the energy budget.
Mwale and Chipofya (2023) implemented a NILM system using a Raspberry Pi 4 and a neural network classifier trained on the REFIT dataset. The system achieved 91 % appliance classification accuracy across 10 categories in laboratory conditions. When the researchers ported the model to an Arduino Due with 96 kilobytes of SRAM ,classification accuracy dropped to 67 % due to quantisation losses. This finding directly motivates the tier classification approach of this research, which avoids neural network inference in favour of threshold-based logic that is fully tractable on an ESP32.
Makonese et al. (2022) reviewed commercial smart energy monitoring adoption across South African households, which represents the closest proxy population to the Zimbabwean residential market. Their study found that cost, internet dependency and installation complexity were the three primary barriers to adoption. Average smart home energy monitor cost in their study was $85 USD for entry level devices with installation costs adding a further $40 USD  to $80 USD. This finding directly informs the $50 USD total bill of materials target for the proposed system.
Heydari and Mahmoud (2025) published a systematic survey of TinyML applications across 200 experimental studies. Their findings confirmed that models constrained to under 100 kilobytes of memory achieve classification accuracy of 82 to 94 % on ARM Cortex-M hardware comparable to the ESP32. The survey identified the residential energy management domain as understudied relative to healthcare and audio processing in the TinyML literature, explicitly noting the absence of validated microcontroller-based residential triage systems. This gap in the TinyML literature is one that this research begins to address.
2.2.3 Emerging Technologies
Recent technological developments offer further enhancement capabilities for residential energy triage systems. Three emerging directions are relevant to the long term evolution of the proposed system.
Federated learning for energy management distributes model training across client devices without transmitting raw consumption data to a central server. Savi and Olivadese (2021) demonstrated that federated NILM models achieved appliance classification accuracy within 5 % age points of centrally trained models while preserving household consumption privacy. For the proposed system ,federated learning represents a compelling future extension: it would enable improving classification thresholds from real Zimbabwean household data across multiple deployed units without any individual household's data leaving the device. Implementation complexity makes this outside the current research scope.
Cloud edge hybrid architectures divide computation between an on-device processor and a cloud analytics server. The edge component handles real time control with low latency. The cloud component provides historical analysis, remote configuration and aggregate insights across multiple installed units. Banbury et al. (2021) demonstrated that hybrid architectures can deliver both the real time response of edge processing and the analytical depth of cloud computing. For the proposed system ,a hybrid architecture would allow Zimbabwean solar installation companies to monitor the energy behaviour of multiple client sites remotely. As with federated learning, this extends beyond the initial scope but represents a natural commercialisation pathway.

2.4 Research Gap
Based on the analysis of existing literature, several specific limitations remain unaddressed in current approaches to residential energy management for the Zimbabwean context.
Most existing systems focus primarily on schedule-based load shedding or post-hoc consumption monitoring, while limited attention has been given to real time, budget responsive, tier aware autonomous shedding on microcontroller class hardware. Diarra et al. (2023) achieved fixed schedules rather than live power classification. Osei-Owusu et al. (2024) demonstrated edge deployable relay control but without any form of load prioritisation.
Additionally, most intelligent approaches rely on cloud connected hardware and large labelled datasets, making them unsuitable for environments with limited computational resources and intermittent connectivity. Mwale and Chipofya (2023) demonstrated that neural network NILM accuracy degrades significantly when constrained to microcontroller hardware. Makonese et al. (2022) confirmed that cost and cloud dependency are the primary barriers to adoption in African residential markets.
Furthermore, no existing validated system in the literature specifically addresses the Zimbabwean residential context, including ZESA prepaid token budget dynamic and local appliance profiles. The TinyML survey of Heydari and Mahmoud (2025) explicitly identifies residential energy management as an understudied domain.
Therefore, there is a need for a system that classifies residential loads into priority tiers in real time on microcontroller class hardware, tracks energy budgets autonomously for prepaid token contexts ,executes graduated triage shedding without any cloud or internet dependency and is validated specifically for Zimbabwean residential electrical conditions. Addressing this gap forms the primary motivation for the present research.
2.5 Proposed Conceptual Solution
Based on the limitations identified in the literature, this research proposes the development of an automated energy triage system for Zimbabwean residential electrical systems. The system uses an ESP32 microcontroller as the edge computing platform, non-invasive CT sensors for load monitoring and relay modules for autonomous circuit control.
The proposed system will integrate edge computing principles, IoT sensor technology, embedded firmware algorithms and local wireless communication. It aims to improve residential energy budget utilisation by enabling graceful load degradation rather than simultaneous power loss across all circuits.
The expected advantages of the proposed system include  improved energy budget preservation through tier aware shedding , enhanced protection of essential appliances during budget critical conditions , reduced dependence on human monitoring and manual intervention and improved battery longevity through proactive triage before hard cutoff thresholds are reached.
The detailed system architecture, component specifications, firmware design and implementation methodology are presented in Chapter 3.

This chapter reviewed existing research related to residential load management, non-intrusive load monitoring, CT based current sensing, edge computing on microcontrollers and relay-based autonomous control. Both traditional and intelligent approaches were examined across five subsections.
The strengths and limitations of existing systems were analysed through a structured comparative framework in Tables 2.1 and 2.2. Traditional systems provide passive protection only. Based on this analysis, a research gap was identified: no existing system combines real time load tier classification, budget aware autonomous shedding and an edge only operation. The next chapter presents the research methodology and detailed system design.

3.1 Research Design
This study adopts a Design Science Research (DSR) approach which I  found to be the most suitable methodology for computing and engineering research that focuses on solving a defined practical problem. Design Science Research is well  established in information systems and computer engineering research because it produces tangible, evaluable artefacts rather than purely theoretical models (Hevner et al., 2004).
Design Science Research focuses on building artefacts, which in this study include the hardware circuit, the ESP32 firmware, the triage algorithm and the monitoring dashboard. These artefacts are designed to solve the identified problem in the  current Zimbabwe’s residential energy management. 
The DSR process followed in this study involves six stages. Stage 1 is Problem Identification, shows the definition of the research problem and its significance in the Zimbabwean residential electrical context. Stage 2 is Definition of Objectives ,  includes identifying the measurable performance requirements the system must satisfy. Stage 3 is Design and Development also includes designing and building the hardware circuit, sensor calibration pipeline, firmware and dashboard. Stage 4 is Demonstration, shows that the developed system detects budget threshold breaches and executes triage shedding correctly. Stage 5 is Evaluation includes assessing performance against the quantitative metrics defined in the research objectives. Lastly , stage 6 is Communication where I present findings and contributions through this dissertation.
The DSR approach is complemented by an empirical experimental method for system evaluation .Controlled experiments measure classification accuracy, relay response latency and budget tracking error against defined hypotheses .This combination of constructive development and experimental evaluation ensures both that the artefact is built correctly and that its performance is measured rigorously.
3.2 Research Methods
3.2.1 Empirical and Experimental Method
The empirical method is used for system evaluation. Controlled experiments are conducted to test each research hypothesis and measure system performance against defined targets. This method is appropriate because the research objectives specify quantitative performance thresholds, such as 85 % classification accuracy and 500 millisecond relay response time, that must be verified through measurement rather than theoretical analysis.
The experimental evaluation is divided into two phases. The exploratory phase calibrates the CT sensing circuit and identifies the power thresholds that best separate load tiers across the tested appliance set. The evaluation phase conducts controlled experiments to answer each research question ,using the calibration established in the exploratory phase. Results are reported with mean values and standard deviations across repeated trials to establish statistical reliability.
3.2.2 Design Science Method
The Design Science method governed the deveopment of a functional ESP32 based energy triage system comprising a hardware prototype circuit, embedded firmware, a triage algorithm and a local Wi-Fi monitoring dashboard. 
It is validated specifically for the Zimbabwean residential electrical context. No prior published system has demonstrated this combination.
3.2.3 System Design and Implementation Method
The system design  implementation used the Arduino framework running on the ESP32 with FreeRTOS task management. System modules are developed incrementally, with each module tested individually before integration. This incremental approach reduces debugging complexity and ensures each objective is addressed before the next is attempted.
3.3 System Architecture
The proposed system follows an IoT based layered edge computing architecture. All processing occurs within the ESP32 and its connected peripherals. There is no dependency on external servers, cloud services or internet connectivity at any stage of system operation. The architecture is divided into four functional layers, as illustrated in Figure 3.1.

3.3.1 Layer 1: Sensing and Control
Layer 1 is the physical hardware layer.Layer 1 consists of the SCT-013 CT sensors, the ESP32 microcontroller and the external relay module. The CT sensors clamp around the live conductors of the monitored circuits and generate proportional AC voltage signals. These signals are fed directly into the ESP32’s internal 12-bit Analog-to-Digital Converters (ADCs), which digitize the waveform for immediate processing hence this  eliminates the need for external conversion modules to keep the BOM cost low.
To guarantee electrical safety and signal integrity, the system employed a physically isolated architecture. Low voltage GPIO control signals are routed from the ESP32 through a 10 pin header to an external, off board relay module. This modular design ensures that all high voltage 220V AC switching occurs entirely off the primary processing board , protecting the delicate ADC components from electromagnetic interference.
3.3.2 Layer 2: Classification
Layer 2 is the signal processing and load classification layer. TThe ESP32 firmware calculates the RMS current directly from the internal ADC samples, derives real power in watts, and applies the tier classification threshold table  to assign each monitored circuit to Tier 1, Tier 2 or Tier 3. This layer operates continuously in a high-priority FreeRTOS task.

3.3.3 Layer 3: Decision Engine
Layer 3 is the triage decision engine. It runs the energy budget tracking module, that will integrate measured power over time to maintain a running estimate of remaining energy in watt-hours. When the remaining budget falls below the configurable critical threshold (default 10 %) ,the decision engine evaluates the tier assignments of all active circuits, identifies the highest active tier and dispatches a relay cut command. If the budget continues to fall after Tier 3 is shed, the engine proceeds to shed Tier 2 loads. Tier 1 loads are never shed under any condition.
3.3.4 Layer 4: Presentation
Layer 4 is the user interface monitoring dashboard. The ESP32 hosts a Wi-Fi access point and an HTTP server on port 80. Any device connected to the ESP32's access point can access the dashboard through a standard mobile browser without any app installation. The dashboard displays real time current readings for each circuit, the assigned tier classification, the remaining energy budget as a percentage gauge, relay state indicators and triage event logs. The dashboard updates every two seconds via a JavaScript polling mechanism.

3.4 Data Collection Methods
Data collection supports both system development and evaluation. Three primary data sources are used in this study.
3.4.1 Literature Review
A systematic literature review was conducted to identify existing technologies, algorithms and systems related to residential load monitoring, edge computing and energy management. Sources were drawn from IEEE Xplore, ACM Digital Library, ScienceDirect, SpringerLink and Google Scholar. At least 60 % of cited sources were published between 2019 and 2026. Wikipedia, blogs and non-peer-reviewed sources were excluded. The review findings are presented in Chapter 2 and directly inform the hardware selection, algorithm design and evaluation methodology.
3.4.2 Sensor Calibration Data
Calibration data is collected by running known resistive loads of defined wattage through the CT sensing circuit and recording the ESP32's measured current values. A certified digital clamp meter (Uni-T UT210E, rated at plus or minus 2 % accuracy) serves as the reference instrument. Ten current levels between 0.5 and 20 amps are tested, with ten repeated readings at each level. The mean measured value, standard deviation and  percentage error relative to the reference are calculated and reported. A linear calibration factor is derived and applied to the ESP32 firmware to minimise systematic measurement error.
3.4.3 Load Classification Test Data

Twelve household appliances representative of the Zimbabwean residential market are selected for classification testing. These include three Tier 1 appliances (refrigerator, router, LED lamp) ,three Tier 2 appliances (television, ceiling fan, phone charger) and six Tier 3 appliances (geyser, electric kettle, microwave oven, clothes iron, washing machine, electric heater). Each appliance is activated individually across three separate trials. The ESP32's tier assignment for each trial is recorded and compared against the ground truth tier assignment to calculate classification accuracy.
3.4.4 System-Generated Evaluation Data

During controlled triage evaluation scenarios, the system generates timestamped log data including: circuit power readings at 2 second intervals, budget remaining  percentage at 2 second intervals, relay state change events with timestamps and triage decision events with the triggering threshold value. This log data is exported from the ESP32 via the Serial Monitor interface and analysed in Python using NumPy and Matplotlib to produce evaluation charts and summary statistics.
3.4.5 User Evaluation Data
Five participants with varying levels of technical background perform three defined monitoring tasks using the dashboard: reading the current energy budget status, identifying which circuit is classified as Tier 3 and confirming that a triage shedding event has occurred. Task completion rate, time-on-task and a five point satisfaction rating are recorded. This qualitative evaluation assesses whether the dashboard provides actionable information without requiring electrical engineering expertise.
3.5 Development Tools and Technologies

3.5.1 Programming Languages and Frameworks
The ESP32 firmware is written in C++ using the Arduino framework with the ESP32 board support package version 2.0.14. FreeRTOS is used for task scheduling. The EmonLib library provides the foundational RMS current calculation functions, extended with custom calibration and tier classification modules. The dashboard is implemented as an inline HTML page served by the ESPAsyncWebServer library. 
Data analysis scripts are written in Python 3.11 using NumPy for numerical computation, Pandas for tabular data handling and Matplotlib for chart generation. The Arduino IDE version 2.3.2 is used as the development environment for ESP32 firmware. Visual Studio Code with the PlatformIO extension is used for larger firmware modules requiring multi-file project management.
3.5.2 Development and Test Equipment

The prototype circuit is assembled on a standard 830 point solderless breadboard during initial development and transferred to a general purpose PCB for evaluation testing. A Uni-T UT210E certified digital clamp meter serves as the reference instrument for calibration. The Arduino IDE's built-in Serial Plotter tool is used to visualize the rea -time analog waveform directly from the ESP32 over a USB serial connection. This allows for visual verification of signal quality and DC bias during calibration without requiring expensive external diagnostic equipment.
The Zimbabwean mains supply frequency is 50 Hz at 230 V RMS nominal. All tests are conducted at the laboratory mains supply at Chinhoyi University of Technology, which operates within the standard tolerance of plus or minus 6 % voltage variation and plus or minus 0.5 Hz frequency variation defined by the Zimbabwe Energy Regulatory Authority (ZERA).
3.6 System Implementation
The implementation follows four sequential phases, each building on the validated outputs of the previous phase.
3.6.1 Phase 1: Hardware Assembly and CT Sensor Calibration

The SCT-013 CT sensors interface with the main board via the standard 3.5 mm TRS sockets. The analog output from the tip of each socket is routed directly to the ESP32’s dedicated internal ADC pins (such as GPIO 34, 35, 36, and 39) to read the sensor data.. Main system power is supplied via a 5V DC barrel jack routed directly to the ESP32's VIN pin. By leveraging the microcontroller's built-in 3.3V regulator the system eliminated the need for external voltage regulation circuits. 

The external relay module is connected via a dedicated 10 pin header to GPIO pins 25, 26, 27 and 32, providing one pin per relay channel alongside shared 5V power and Ground . A 10μF capacitor and a burden resistor are added to each CT sensor output to improve signal quality and establish a 1.65V DC bias midpoint. This offset ensures the AC waveform sits perfectly in the center of the ESP32's 0-3.3V internal ADC reading range, preventing signal clipping.

The SCT-013 sensor was clamped around the live wire only. The thick outer cable jacket was separated to reach the inner wires. The inner coloured insulation was not cut or stripped. This kept the system safe from electrical shocks. The live and neutral wires were never clamped together. If both wires were clamped, their magnetic fields cancelled each other out and the sensor read zero.
Calibration is performed by passing known currents through the CT sensor and recording the ESP32's raw ADC values .A linear regression is performed on the calibration data to derive the calibration coefficient applied in the firmware. Calibration is repeated after any hardware modification to ensure measurement integrity is maintained throughout the evaluation phase.
3.6.2 Phase 2: Firmware Development
The firmware is structured as four FreeRTOS tasks.Task 1, the Sampling Task, runs on Core 0 at the highest priority. It will continuously poll the ESP32's internal ADC registers to capture the current waveform. Task 2, the Classification Task, runs on Core 0 at medium priority and computes RMS current, derives power and assigns tier classifications every 500 milliseconds. Task 3, the Budget Task, runs on Core 1 at high priority and updates the energy budget estimate every two seconds and executes triage logic when the threshold is breached. Task 4, the Dashboard Task, runs on Core 1 at low priority and handles HTTP requests for the monitoring interface.
The tier classification thresholds are stored in a configurable constant array in the firmware header file. Default values are 0 to 200 watts for Tier 1, 200 to 800 watts for Tier 2 and above 800 watts for Tier 3. These thresholds can be adjusted through the dashboard configuration interface without flashing the firmware.
3.6.3 Phase 3: Integration and Unit Testing
Each firmware module is tested independently before integration. The Sampling Task is tested by visualizing the real time ADC readings using the Arduino Serial Plotter to confirm a clean, centered sinusoidal waveform for a known test load.The Classification Task is tested by activating individual appliances and confirming correct tier assignment against the ground-truth tier table. The Budget Task is tested using a simulated linear depletion rate and confirming that triage commands are dispatched at the correct remaining budget  percentage. The Dashboard Task is tested by connecting a mobile browser to the ESP32 access point and confirming that all displayed values match the Serial Monitor output.
3.6.4 Phase 4: System Integration
All four tasks are integrated into the final firmware build and tested as a complete system. Two end-to-end triage scenarios are run: a ZESA prepaid token depletion scenario and a combined high consumption scenario where multiple Tier 3 loads are active simultaneously. Each scenario is run five times to assess consistency. System integration testing confirms that all modules interact correctly, that relay commands are executed in the correct tier order and that Tier 1 loads remain powered throughout all test scenarios.
3.7 System Testing and Evaluation
3.7.1 Unit Testing
Individual modules are tested in isolation to verify functional correctness before integration. The CT sensing and RMS calculation module is verified against the reference clamp meter across ten current levels. The tier classification module is verified against the ground-truth tier table for all 12 test appliances. The budget tracking module is verified against manually calculated energy consumption values for three 30-minute test runs. The relay control module is verified by confirming GPIO signal timing against the relay switching event using the oscilloscope.
3.7.2 Integration Testing
System modules are combined and tested to ensure they interact correctly under real operating conditions. Integration tests confirm that ADC samples flow correctly to the RMS calculation module, that tier assignments update within the 500millisecond classification cycle, that budget updates trigger triage commands at the correct threshold level and that relay state changes are reflected in the dashboard within two seconds of the event.

Load classification accuracy is calculated as the number of correct tier assignments divided by the total number of classification events across all 12 test appliances and three repeated trials. 3.8 Ethical Considerations
This research involves human participants in the dashboard usability evaluation described in Section 3.7.5. Informed consent is obtained from all five participants before the evaluation session. Participants are informed of the purpose of the study, the tasks they will be asked to perform and their right to withdraw at any time without consequence. No personally identifiable information is collected. Task performance data is anonymised and reported as aggregate statistics only.
The hardware prototype handles 230V AC mains voltage on the load-side relay circuits. All high-voltage testing is conducted in the CUT electrical engineering laboratory under direct supervision, with appropriate isolation precautions and access to emergency isolation switches. The ESP32 control circuit operates at 5V DC and 3.3V DC only. No student or participant is exposed to high voltage conductors during any evaluation session.
The research does not involve sensitive personal data, financial information or vulnerable populations. No data is transmitted to external servers at any point. All evaluation data is stored locally on the researcher's password protected device and deleted after the dissertation is submitted.
3.9 Limitations of the Methodology
Limited Calibration Range. The CT sensor calibration is performed across ten current levels between 0.5Amps  and 20Amps . Loads above 20 amps, which include large three phase geysers and workshop equipment ,are outside the validated calibration range. Measurement accuracy for currents above 20 amps is extrapolated from the calibration curve rather than directly validated.
Small Usability Sample. The dashboard usability evaluation uses five participants, which provides indicative but not statistically significant results. A larger sample of 20 to 30 participants would be required to draw generalisable conclusions about usability for the broader Zimbabwean residential market. The five participant sample is appropriate for a formative evaluation within the resource constraints of this dissertation.
Simulated Budget Depletion. The energy budget tracking is evaluated using mathematically modelled depletion scenarios rather than live integration with a ZESA prepaid meter. The simulated model assumes a fixed starting balance and a depletion rate calculated from measured current consumption. Real prepaid meters introduce additional variables including tariff rate changes and demand charges that are not modelled in the simulation.
Laboratory Test Environment. All evaluation is conducted in a controlled laboratory environment at CUT. Electromagnetic interference, wiring impedances and current harmonics present in real residential installations may affect CT sensor accuracy beyond the levels observed in the laboratory. Field validation in a real residential installation is identified as future work.
